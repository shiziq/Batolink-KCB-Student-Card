## ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS & RESPONSES

**Q: "Why can't M-Pesa just build this?"**
A: "M-Pesa's DNA is digital-to-digital. Building a physical POS network, card issuance infrastructure, and merchant acquiring ecosystem would take them 2-3 years. By then, Batolink and KCB would have locked in merchant relationships, customer habits, and network effects. M-Pesa is our partner, not our competitor — their wallet users are our best customers."

**Q: "What about the CBK regulatory framework?"**
A: "Batolink operates as a technology layer, not a deposit-taking institution. All funds sit in KCB accounts. CBK oversight flows through KCB's existing license. We've had preliminary discussions and they view this as bank-led innovation that advances financial inclusion."

**Q: "These projections seem aggressive."**
A: "Actually conservative. We based them on 15% adoption in year one, not the 40-50% we've seen in comparable fintech launches. The KSh 2.4B assumes only Nairobi-level transaction density. Rural expansion could double it. And remember — every number you see is traceable to a per-transaction fee of KSh 8.08."

**Q: "What's stopping a competitor from copying this?"**
A: "First-mover advantage in distribution. Merchant relationships are sticky — once a petrol station has our POS, switching costs are high. Customer habits form quickly — the card in their wallet becomes their default payment method. And the data moat — transaction history for credit scoring — compounds over time. By the time a competitor reacts, we'll have thousands of POS devices and millions of cardholders."

**Q: "What are the real risks?"**
A: "Three risks, all manageable. Technology risk: our prototype is already working — this isn't vaporware. Adoption risk: that's why the first three months are free. If customers don't adopt, KCB walks away with zero cost. Regulatory risk: we operate within KCB's existing license. The biggest risk is actually inaction — because if we don't do this, someone else will."
